Public Infrastructure: Description back to ToC

The Public Infrastructure ontology represents data on the facilities (equipment) of a municipality. These include educational, social, and cultural centers, sports facilities, as well as on- and off-street parking and shared bicycle stations. The ontology reuses concepts related to sensors (Semantic Sensor Network Ontology) to represent the access of people and vehicles and concepts from the Organization Ontology (The Organization Ontology) to represent the entities that manage the equipment. Geospatial concepts are also reused through the GeoSPARQL ontology to represent areas or coordinates of the facility and some of its parts. Additionally, the Smart Applications REFerence Ontology (SAREF) is referenced to represent the concept of Building.

As shown in Figure 1, there is a main concept in the ontology, MunicipalFacility, which corresponds to a facility that a citizen can access and that belongs to a local government.

The Municipal Facility class is equivalent to the class Building, which is part of the vertical domain S4BLDG of the SAREF ontology. This ontology allows for the representation of concepts related to the management of resources (energy and others) of a building. The class OffStreetParking is another relevant type of facility and will be explained in detail later on. Finally, the class EstacionBicicleta represents shared bicycle stations managed by local authorities. The vocabulary used for representing data on the public bicycle system (esbici) is reused.

A facility can offer certain regular activities (offersRegularActivity), for example, a cultural center can offer concerts, exhibitions, etc. It can also provide certain public services, such as internet access via Wi-Fi (providesPublicService). The facility is managed (rolePlayedBy) by an ExternalOrganization or a MunicipalService. This organization can play different types of roles (hasManagementRoleType) such as security or maintenance. Different types of sensors (AccessSensor) can be located on the facility, which monitor (AccessObservation) certain properties such as vehicle or pedestrian access. An example is LPR cameras located at parking lot entrances. A facility has a postal address (address) and also generally belongs to (belongsTo) a neighborhood or district of a municipality. The vocabulary for postal address (esdir) and the vocabulary representing administrative divisions in Spain (esadm) have been reused.

A facility can contain other facilities (containsFacility). This relationship allows for the representation of different areas within a facility. For example, a cultural center might have auditoriums and exhibition halls; a sports center might have a soccer field or a swimming pool. Each facility can have an (equipment type) that corresponds to a multi-level taxonomy defined for the previously developed Municipal Equipment vocabulary esequip.

Figure 2 develops the concept of Parking.

A parking area can be an OffRoadParking that includes parking facilities or surfaces off public roads, or an OnRoadParking that includes parking on streets or avenues that can be identified for residents, non-residents, free parking, loading and unloading, among others. These parking areas can be part of a RegulatedParkingZone. A parking area can have a ParkingFee. A parking lot has groups of slots by vehicle type (ParkingSpotsByVehicleType), for example, cars, motorcycles, and bicycles. It also has groups of spaces by permit type (ParkingSpotsByPermitType), such as residents, people with reduced mobility, businesses, loading and unloading, among others. Parking lots located off public roads have access points (ParkingAccess) for vehicle entry and exit. These access points also have a postal address and other relevant information.
